Toronto's Top Lofts

The term "loft" is subjective in Toronto. There's loft-inspired, and then there's true conversion — brick and beam, mostly early 1900s warehouses repurposed into one-of-a-kind residences. With virtually no new supply, these buildings represent an irreplaceable piece of the city's architectural heritage.

Toy Factory Lofts

43 Hanna Ave · Liberty Village

Arguably the number one address in Liberty Village. The former Irwin Toy factory, built in the early 1900s, was converted by Lanterra in 2008. The original floors feature exposed red brick, Douglas fir ceilings, and cast iron beams, while the extension offers 12.5-foot concrete ceilings with balconies. Renowned for its superior management and uniquely convenient same-floor parking.

Candy Factory Lofts

993 Queen St W · Queen West

Built in the 1930s as the DC Candy plant, converted in 1999 by Harry Stinson — the pioneer of Toronto's warehouse-to-condo movement. This was the very first building to start the loft conversion trend in the city. The smallest unit starts at 980 sq ft, with layouts reaching up to 4,500 sq ft. Pure hard loft from ground to roof, with top-floor units offering rooftop terrace access via spiral staircase.

Broadview Lofts

68 Broadview Ave · Riverdale

A former Rexall pharmacy warehouse from the early 1900s, converted in 2006. Widely regarded as one of the most beautiful loft buildings in the city, featuring beige concrete brick, wood ceilings, and polished concrete floors. The penthouse extensions boast 20-foot ceilings and glass box windows — each one truly unique. Some units include separately deeded private outdoor terraces.

Robert Watson Lofts

363–369 Sorauren Ave · Roncesvalles

Another early 1900s warehouse — this one formerly produced renowned mints. Located in the heart of Roncesvalles, one of Toronto's most desirable and condo-scarce neighbourhoods. The extension units stand out with almost entirely bi-level and multi-level layouts, a rarity in the condo market. Units top out around 1,500 sq ft with ground-level walkout options available.

Chocolate Company Lofts

955 Queen St W · Queen West

Originally the Patterson Candy Co. factory built in 1912, converted by Plazacorp in 2005. Two heritage buildings linked by a modern six-storey addition house 144 lofts blending hard and soft styles. Hard loft units feature 12-foot wood or corrugated metal ceilings with exposed brick, while soft lofts offer 10.5-foot ceilings and floor-to-ceiling glass. Directly across from Trinity Bellwoods Park.

Massey Harris Lofts

915 King St W · King West

Once home to the legendary Massey-Harris agricultural equipment company, this striking red brick heritage building on King West has been transformed into one of the neighbourhood's most distinctive loft residences. The beautifully preserved Victorian-era industrial façade features arched windows and ornamental brickwork, with a modern rooftop addition above. A rare piece of Canadian industrial history reimagined for contemporary living.

The Kings Lofts

800 King St W · King West

Built in 2000 by Triloft Developments, this boutique five-storey building houses 76 refreshingly spacious soft lofts ranging from 700 to 1,100 sq ft. Many units feature true two-storey configurations with 18-foot ceilings, double-height windows, and open mezzanine bedrooms. Exposed concrete, hardwood floors, BBQ-friendly balconies, and a prime King West location right at Niagara Street.

Abbey Lane Lofts

261 King St E · St. Lawrence

An intimate 29-unit boutique building constructed in 2003 by Harhay in the St. Lawrence neighbourhood. This purpose-built soft loft features heritage-style red brick architecture with exposed concrete, industrial ductwork, and warehouse windows. Ceilings soar from 11 to a staggering 20 feet in the split-level penthouse suites. Steps from St. Lawrence Market and the Distillery District.
